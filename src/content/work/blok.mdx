<CaseSection id="context">

<CaseSubsection title="Fragmented organisation">

Sitecore was on a mission to expand its ageing enterprise CMS offering to a full digital experience platform, and challenge category leader Adobe.

To fill out its platform, Sitecore rapidly acquired 6 products, and started building several new ones.

The resulting organisation was a patchwork of companies, with distributed teams, using different technologies, working in silos.

</CaseSubsection>

<CaseSection id="problem">

<CaseSubsection title="Unify the experience">

Customers experienced a disjointed platform: a dozen different products, with no shared identity or design language. A far cry from the envisioned unified ‘DXP’.

My internal framing of the challenge:

<FigmaEmbed src="https://embed.figma.com/proto/TKdfHvEfinvG1mdhr7AtpD?scaling=scale-down-width&embed-host=share" title="Design challenge" />

</CaseSubsection>

<CaseSubsection title="Inefficiency">

Internally, every team paid a tax on custom UI.

We needed cohesive UX at scale and engineering efficiency.

{/* We needed to bring these islands together and create a cohesive platform user experience, all while accelerating development by reducing waste and duplication. */}

</CaseSubsection>

<CaseSubsection title="Fix the design system">

I identified the **design system** as the highest-leverage problem I could solve; not a single product, but the building blocks for all products.

However, I had no budget, no team, and no formal mandate. I had to make the case and build momentum from scratch.

</CaseSubsection>

</CaseSection>

<CaseSection id="approach">

<CaseSubsection title="Discovery">

There had been a previous attempt at a design system, but adoption was low. Developer interviews were clear: the existing tools were hard to use, so teams skipped them. We were not getting the intended quality or speed gains.

{/* I interviewed developers across teams and found our existing UI kit was too limiting and they spent more time fighting it than benefiting from it. */}

</CaseSubsection>

<CaseSubsection title="Reboot">

I decided to reboot entirely. The new design system needed to be easy to use, flexible, and prioritise the developer experience.

I recruited volunteer developers from various teams. Together we built the foundation. I learned enough React to fill the gaps myself.

A few months later, **Blok Design System** was born: a React library on NPM, with a comprehensive documentation site, design tokens, Figma kits, content guidelines, coding standards, and brand assets.

<CaseSubsection title="Roll-out">

To kickstart adoption, I ran an evangelisation campaign: Town Hall presentations, a Sitecore user conference talk, internal blog posts, and hands-on migration support for teams.

<CaseSection id="outcome">

<CaseSubsection title="Faster and better delivery">

Blok became the shared language between Design and Engineering. In one year, Blok was **adopted by 12+ teams**.

Engineers reported **faster delivery** with cleaner code and fewer UI defects;

- “It’s cool how quickly you can get results.”
- “Lot less focus for us on making it look right.”
- “We can focus more on building functionality and providing value.”
- “Much cleaner and more maintainable code.”
- “It saved us tons of time.”
- “We were able to close a lot of QA tickets.”

</CaseSubsection>

<CaseSection id="reflections">

<CaseSubsection title="No code, no design system">

 The impact of a design system is only realised when it helps developers build the end product.

 This project solidified my belief that **you don’t really have a design system unless you have reusable code.**

</CaseSubsection>

<CaseSubsection title="If you build it, they will come">

This experience had me launch a **zero-to-one project** without resources, by generating internal momentum and attracting contributors. Once the foundation was there, its value became undeniable and gravity brought more people on board.

It has taught me that **there is always a way**, and that **relationships are everything**.

</CaseSubsection>
