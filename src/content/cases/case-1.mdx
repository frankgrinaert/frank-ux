<CaseSection id="context">

<CaseSubsection title="Fragmented organisation">

Sitecore was on a mission to expand its ageing enterprise CMS offering to a full digital experience platform — a category dominated by Adobe.

To fill out its product suite, Sitecore rapidly acquired 6 companies, and started numerous zero-to-one products.

The result: Sitecore was a patchwork of smaller companies, distributed teams, on different tech stacks, working in isolation from each other.

<CaseSection id="problem">

<CaseSubsection title="Unify the experience">

The disjointed organization translated to a disjointed user experience: a dozen different products, with no shared identity or design language. A far cry from the unified digital experience platform we were aiming for.
{/* 
Not to mention the wasted effort of designing and building the same UI patterns over and over again. */}

We needed to bring these islands together and create a cohesive user experience across the platform, all while accelerating development by reducing waste and duplication.

</CaseSubsection>

<CaseSubsection title="Fix the design system">

I identified the design system as the highest-leverage problem I could solve; not a single product, but the building blocks for all products.

However, because a design system isn’t itself a commercial product, it wasn’t on anybody’s priority list. I had no budget, no team, and no formal mandate — even a fair bit of opposition. I had to make the case and build momentum from scratch.

</CaseSubsection>

</CaseSection>

<CaseSection id="approach">

<CaseSubsection title="Discovery">

I interviewed developers across teams and found our existing UI kit was too limiting and they spent more time fighting it than benefiting from it.

</CaseSubsection>

<CaseSubsection title="Reboot">

To address the pain points, I made the decision to reboot entirely. The new design system needed to be easy to use, flexible, and prioritise the developer experience.

I recruited passionate dev volunteers from various teams. Together we built the foundation. I learned enough React to fill the gaps myself.

A few months later, the new design system was born: a full documentation site, with React components, design tokens, Figma libraries, content guidelines, coding standards, and brand assets.

I called it **Blok**.

</CaseSubsection>

<CaseSubsection title="Roll-out">

With the design system launched, now we needed to increase adoption.

I ran a sustained evangelisation campaign: Town Hall presentations, a Sitecore user conference talk, internal blog posts, and hands-on migration support for teams.

12+ teams adopted Blok. 

</CaseSubsection>

</CaseSection>

<CaseSection id="outcome">

<CaseSubsection title="Faster dev">

Developer feedback was great:
- “It’s cool how quickly you can get results.”
- “Lot less focus for us on making it look right.”
- “We can focus more on building functionality and providing value.”
- “Much cleaner and more maintainable code.”
- “It saved us tons of time.”
- “We were able to close a lot of QA tickets.”

</CaseSubsection>

<CaseSubsection title="Consistent UX">

Blok became the shared language between Design and Engineering.

Our diverse products now felt like they belonged together.

</CaseSubsection>

</CaseSection>

<CaseSection id="reflections">

<CaseSubsection title="If you build it, they will come">

This experience had me launch a **zero-to-one project** without resources, by generating internal momentum and attracting contributors. Once the foundation was built, its value became undeniable and gravity brought more people on board.

It has taught me that **there is always a way**, and that **relationships are everything**.

</CaseSubsection>

<CaseSubsection title="UI kit ≠ design system">

 The impact of a design system is only realised when it is used by developers.

 Your developers are your end users. This project solidified my belief that **you don’t really have a design system unless you have reusable code.**

</CaseSubsection>
