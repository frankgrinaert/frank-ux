<CaseSection id="context">

<CaseSubsection title="Midway through the portal">

While building the <a href="/work/cloud-portal">Cloud Portal &amp; app switcher</a>, the suite finally had a front door — but the homepage was still taking shape in beta. Many stakeholders, no single strategy, and pressure to promote every product on the landing experience.

</CaseSubsection>

</CaseSection>

<CaseSection id="problem">

<CaseSubsection title="Homepage syndrome">

The portal fell into [homepage syndrome](https://youtu.be/99e6d2purlo?t=300) — every group lobbied for tiles, banners, and promos. Early-access users felt overwhelmed and disoriented; they could not find their next task.

The risk was not polish alone — it was that the portal would fail as the **operating layer** for the suite and undermine the unification narrative the portal was meant to deliver.

</CaseSubsection>

</CaseSection>

<CaseSection id="approach">

<CaseSubsection title="Course-correct with evidence">

I did not argue from opinion. I ran an **early-access interview programme** with real customers, synthesised findings, and presented them to Product Management: users were overloaded; prized features were invisible in practice.

That secured buy-in for a significant simplification. I paired with engineering to implement a homepage scoped to jobs-to-be-done, not stakeholder count.

<CaseSection id="outcome">

<CaseSubsection title="A homepage that works">

We shipped a focused Cloud Portal homepage — still the suite entry built in the <a href="/work/cloud-portal">portal case</a>, but easier to orient and act from.

<img
  src="/work/portal-shipped.png"
  alt="Shipped Cloud Portal — focused homepage and apps grid"
  className="w-full rounded-lg"
/>

**Product:** Homepage scope tied to what users need to do next, not how many teams wanted a tile.

**Process:** Research was used to **challenge** leadership direction, not only validate it.

</CaseSubsection>

</CaseSection>

<CaseSection id="reflections">

<CaseSubsection title="Debate with data">

Concrete customer evidence is more persuasive than theoretical arguments. Run it early, before opinions harden and sunk cost makes simplification politically expensive.

</CaseSubsection>

<CaseSubsection title="Make platform work tangible">

Ambiguous, multi-stakeholder work needs iterative prototypes and shared evidence when priorities diverge. The portal taught me that stakeholder management is a core design skill for platform UX.

</CaseSubsection>

</CaseSection>
