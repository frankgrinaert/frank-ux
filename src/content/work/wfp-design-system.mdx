<CaseSection id="context">

<CaseSubsection title="UX at humanitarian scale">

The World Food Programme runs a large portfolio of **100+ internal IT solutions**. Most are built and maintained by **external development partners**, with limited UX oversight.

Without a design system teams actually use, every product reinvents interface patterns. Accessibility drifts, QA churn grows, and engineering time disappears into one-off styling.

The business stake is simple: **one credible foundation for quality and speed of delivery** across the IT portfolio.

</CaseSubsection>

</CaseSection>

<CaseSection id="problem">

<CaseSubsection title="A system that worked against delivery">

My audit of the legacy design system showed it was outdated, buried in tech debt, and painful to use and maintain. Worse: there was **no dedicated team** to evolve it.

Developers worked around it; hacks, duplicated components, slow releases.

Accessibility was inconsistent.

</CaseSubsection>

</CaseSection>

<CaseSection id="approach">

<CaseSubsection title="Make the case to start fresh">

Through internal presentations, I spelled out the cost of staying on the old foundation. I ran workshops with the architects to benchmark modern frontend approaches, and we reached agreement: **reboot**, not incremental patch.

</CaseSubsection>

<CaseSubsection title="Zero to one">

I hired a frontend engineer and led a **zero-to-one** programme: a new code-first foundation shipped in roughly **two months**.

<CaseSection id="reflections">

<CaseSubsection title="Read the room">

I underestimated sensitivities around the old system; sunk-cost attachment and pride in work that had clearly stopped scaling. I moved fast, delivered the new foundation in record time, and got the technical outcome. I also strained relationships by bulldozing past resistance instead of bringing more people along earlier.

The lesson parallels <a href="/work/blok">Blok</a>: you do not have a design system without reusable code developers trust; but at WFP I am learning that *how* you retire the old one matters as much as *what* you ship next.

</CaseSubsection>
